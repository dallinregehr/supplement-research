1. Creatine Monohydrate
5g/day · any time, just be consistent
Taking ▼
The single most well-studied sports supplement. Beyond strength gains, meta-analyses show cognitive benefits (improved processing speed and memory) and protective effects on bone density and lean mass with aging. Continue 5g/day — you're doing this right.
Evidence strength
Strong
Skeptic's caveat
Creatine is the rare supplement that survives scrutiny. The main legitimate concern — kidney damage — has been thoroughly debunked. A 2025 meta-analysis in BMC Nephrology confirmed that creatine causes a modest rise in serum creatinine (a metabolic byproduct, not kidney damage), with no significant changes in GFR. However, caution is warranted for those with pre-existing kidney conditions, as evidence in that population is lacking. The cognitive benefits, while real, are modest and mostly demonstrated in sleep-deprived or vegetarian populations — the effect size in well-rested omnivores may be smaller than headlines suggest.

2. Vitamin D₃ + K2
~3,000 IU D3 + ~200μg K2 · via AG1 stack
Covered ▼
Your AG1 powder (~1,000 IU) + 2 D3+K2 drops (2,000 IU D3, 200μg K2-MK7) puts you squarely in the evidence-based optimal range. D3 reduces cancer mortality and respiratory infection risk; K2 directs calcium to bones instead of arteries. No changes needed — just confirm with a 25(OH)D blood test.
Evidence strength
Mixed
Skeptic's caveat
The VITAL trial (25,871 participants, NEJM 2022) — the largest RCT of vitamin D supplementation — found no benefit for cancer incidence, cardiovascular events, or fracture prevention in healthy adults not selected for deficiency. Critically, ~40% of the control group was already taking vitamin D, diluting the comparison. An NEJM editorial called the results a "decisive verdict" against routine supplementation in replete adults. The benefits appear real only for people who are actually deficient (25(OH)D < 20 ng/mL). Since you eat well and may get sun exposure, your levels might already be fine without any supplementation — which is exactly why bloodwork matters more than a default dose. K2 evidence is thinner: the arterial stiffness RCT is a single study, and most K2 cardiovascular evidence is observational.

3. Omega-3 Fish Oil
1–2g EPA+DHA · morning with fat
Add ▼
Biggest gap in your current stack. Meta-analysis of 127K+ participants shows reduced cardiovascular risk with dose-response relationship. AG1 contains zero omega-3s. Benefits strongest in people with low baseline intake or existing CVD risk factors.
Evidence strength
Mixed
Skeptic's caveat
The picture here is more complicated than the supplement industry suggests. The STRENGTH trial (AHA 2020) found that high-dose omega-3 carboxylic acids provided no cardiovascular benefit over corn oil placebo — and increased atrial fibrillation risk. A meta-analysis in Circulation found omega-3 supplementation was associated with a 37% increased risk of atrial fibrillation (IRR 1.37, 95% CI 1.22–1.54), with a dose-dependent relationship: doses >1g/day showed a 49% increase vs. 12% at ≤1g/day. The VITAL trial also found no significant CVD benefit from omega-3s in healthy adults. The positive meta-analyses are heavily influenced by the REDUCE-IT trial (icosapent ethyl), which used a mineral oil placebo that may have inflated the treatment effect. For a healthy 40-year-old with no CVD risk factors who eats fish occasionally, the net benefit of supplementation is genuinely uncertain. If you do supplement, staying at ≤1g/day appears to minimize the AFib signal.

200–400mg elemental · evening before bed
Add ▼
Involved in 300+ enzymatic reactions. Subclinical deficiency is widespread. RCTs show improved sleep quality in adults 35–55. Supports muscle recovery, blood pressure regulation, and glucose metabolism. AG1 has some magnesium but likely not enough of the right form.
Evidence strength
Moderate
Skeptic's caveat
Magnesium is a social media darling that has outrun its evidence base. Harvard Health (2025) notes that many magnesium sleep studies are "quite small" with "inconsistent results," and that the evidence for sleep benefits in people without deficiency is weak. McGill's Office for Science and Society states plainly that magnesium "does not perform miracles" and that a healthy diet with fruits, vegetables, and whole grains provides sufficient amounts. The "300+ enzymatic reactions" talking point, while biochemically true, doesn't mean supplementation helps — those reactions require adequate magnesium, not excess. A Tufts review concludes that unless you have a diagnosed or suspected deficiency, there's no compelling reason to supplement. If you eat nuts, seeds, leafy greens, and whole grains regularly, you may already be replete. This is the supplement most likely to be unnecessary if you genuinely "eat pretty well."

5. Collagen Peptides
15g · pre-training with vitamin C
Add ▼
Tendons and joints are the limiting factor for long-term lifting consistency in your 40s. Meta-analysis shows improved tendon morphology and maximal strength when combined with exercise. Evidence is promising but still maturing (GRADE: very low certainty).
Evidence strength
Weak
Skeptic's caveat
This is where skepticism is most warranted. The GRADE certainty for the key Sports Medicine meta-analysis was rated "very low" — the weakest possible rating. A 2025 meta-analysis in The American Journal of Medicine revealed a critical finding: studies not funded by supplement companies showed no significant effect, while industry-funded studies did — a textbook signal of publication/funding bias. Additionally, whey protein was shown to stimulate muscle protein synthesis more effectively than collagen peptides, and one study found no difference in muscle collagen synthesis between collagen and whey — suggesting exercise itself, not the protein type, drives the benefit. Harvard Health notes that your body breaks collagen peptides into amino acids just like any other protein. At 15g/day, you may simply be buying expensive glycine and proline that a varied diet already provides.

7. Ashwagandha
300–600mg KSM-66 · evening, cycled
Optional ▼
Solid evidence for cortisol reduction and modest testosterone increase. AG1 already contains some ashwagandha (likely subtherapeutic). Only worth adding separately if stress or testosterone is a specific concern. Cycle 8 weeks on, 2–4 weeks off.
Evidence strength
Moderate
Skeptic's caveat
Emerging hepatotoxicity signal. The NIH LiverTox database now documents 23 cases of clinically apparent liver injury attributed to ashwagandha, typically presenting 2–12 weeks after initiation with cholestatic hepatitis. While rare, cases include fatal liver failure and emergent liver transplantation, particularly in patients with pre-existing liver disease. A 2023 case series from India in Hepatology Communications documented additional cases. A 2025 AI-driven meta-analysis in Frontiers in Nutrition concluded the risk is minimal at recommended doses for the general population, but the signal is real. Additionally, the testosterone increase (14.7%) was demonstrated in overweight males aged 40–70 in a single small crossover study — this is far from robust evidence. The cortisol reduction is better supported, but whether lowering cortisol in a healthy, non-chronically-stressed person is beneficial (vs. interfering with normal HPA axis function) is an open question. If you take this, get liver enzymes checked periodically.
